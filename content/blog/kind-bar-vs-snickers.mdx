It's a familiar moment at the gas station: a Kind Bar in your left hand, a Snickers in your right. One feels virtuous, one feels like dessert. But once you flip the wrappers over, the numbers tell a more complicated story.

## The lineup

We're comparing the standard sizes you'd actually buy:

- **Kind Dark Chocolate Nuts & Sea Salt bar** — 40 g
- **Snickers original** — 52.7 g (the "regular" size, not Fun Size)

## Side-by-side

| Metric         | Kind Bar (40 g) | Snickers (52.7 g) |
| -------------- | --------------- | ----------------- |
| Calories       | 180             | 250               |
| Total fat      | 13 g            | 12 g              |
| Saturated fat  | 3.5 g           | 4.5 g             |
| Total sugars   | 5 g             | 27 g              |
| Added sugars   | 4 g             | 27 g              |
| Protein        | 6 g             | 4 g               |
| Fiber          | 3 g             | 1 g               |
| Sodium         | 125 mg          | 120 mg            |

## What actually matters

**Sugar is the headline difference.** A Snickers carries roughly **5x the sugar** of a Kind bar — 27 g vs 5 g. That's about 7 teaspoons in a single bar, more than the AHA's full daily added-sugar limit for women (25 g).

**Fat is nearly identical**, but the *composition* differs. Kind's fat comes mostly from almonds and peanuts (mono- and polyunsaturated). Snickers' fat is mostly from milk fat and palm oil — heavier on saturated fat.

**Protein and fiber tilt to Kind.** 6 g vs 4 g of protein, 3 g vs 1 g of fiber. Both meaningfully change how full you feel an hour later.

## The verdict

If you're choosing between the two as a once-in-a-while snack, neither is going to derail you. But as a *habitual* choice — the bar you grab three times a week from your office stash — the difference compounds:

- **Kind bar** ≈ 12 g added sugar/week
- **Snickers** ≈ 81 g added sugar/week

That's a ~70 g/week gap in added sugar, which over a year is the equivalent of about 14 cups of sugar.

## The insight most people miss

Kind isn't health food. Five grams of added sugar is still added sugar, and "13 g of fat" isn't trivial. The honest framing is: a Kind bar is a **better candy bar**, not a **healthy snack**. If you're treating it as the latter, you're double-counting it — eating it *plus* the snack you actually wanted.

The same logic applies to a lot of "better-for-you" packaged foods. The label looks clean, the marketing is calm and beige, and the assumption follows. But the only honest move is to read the nutrition panel, not the front of the box.
